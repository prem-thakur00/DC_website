1. Analog vs Digital Signals

📌 What is a Signal?
A signal is an electrical or electromagnetic wave that carries information. It translates data into a physical form that can be transmitted through a medium like cables or airwaves.
🔵 Analog Signal
Definition: Analog signals are continuous signals that can take any value within a range. They vary smoothly over time with infinite possible values, like the volume of sound or the brightness of light.

Example: An analog clock with continuously moving hands, or your voice when speaking.

🟢 Digital Signal
Definition: Digital signals have discrete values (usually two levels: 0 and 1 in binary). They change abruptly between fixed levels, like a light switch that is either ON or OFF.

Example: A digital clock showing exact time, or binary data (0s and 1s) in computer memory.

💡 Key Insight: Analog signals are smoother and continuous but fragile. Digital signals are robust and can tolerate interference, making them ideal for long-distance communication.

2. Periodic and Non-Periodic Signals

Periodic Signal: A signal that repeats its pattern after a fixed time interval called the period. One complete repetition is called a cycle.

Non-Periodic Signal: A signal that doesn't exhibit a repeating pattern over time. It changes without returning to the same state.

⚙️ Periodic vs Non-Periodic Simulator

Value: 1.5 sec
Time to complete one cycle

🎯 Observation Tips:
  • Periodic signals: Notice the repetition - same pattern repeats
  • Non-periodic signals: Random changes without repeating pattern
  • Frequency = 1 / Period
Key Formulas:
Period (T) = Time for one complete cycle (seconds)
Frequency (F) = 1 / T (in Hertz - cycles per second)
T = 1 / F
💡 Real-World Example: Power supply in India is 50 Hz, meaning it completes 50 cycles per second. The period is T = 1/50 = 0.02 seconds (20 milliseconds).

3. Wavelength Concept

Wavelength (λ): The physical length of one complete cycle of a wave as it travels through a medium. It's the distance a wave travels during one period.
Wavelength Formula:
λ = c / f

where:
λ = Wavelength (in meters)
c = Speed of light in vacuum ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s
f = Frequency (in Hz)

4. Time Domain vs Frequency Domain

Time Domain: Signal representation showing how signal amplitude changes over time. This is what you see on an oscilloscope - the natural way we observe signals.

Frequency Domain: Signal representation showing what frequencies are present in the signal and their amplitudes. This is what you see on a spectrum analyzer.

5. Composite Signals

Composite Signal: A signal made by combining multiple sine waves with different frequencies, amplitudes, and phases. This is essential for transmitting complex data like voice, video, and music.
📌 Fourier Principle: Any complex periodic signal can be represented as a sum of simple sine waves at different frequencies. This is called Fourier decomposition.

6. Transmission Impairments: Attenuation, Distortion & Noise

Transmission Impairment: The degradation of a signal as it travels through a transmission medium. Three main types are attenuation, distortion, and noise.
🔻 Attenuation (Loss of Signal Strength)
Definition: Attenuation is the loss of signal energy as it travels through the transmission medium. The signal becomes weaker over distance.

Solution: Use amplifiers or repeaters to boost the signal strength.

⚠️ Impact on Communication:
  • Attenuation → Signals fade over distance, need amplification
  • Distortion → Signal shape changes, harder to recognize
  • Noise → Random interference confuses the signal
  • Together → Make error detection and correction critical!

7. Performance Metrics of Data Communication

Performance Metrics: Quantitative measures to evaluate how well a communication system transmits data. These help compare different networks and systems.
📌 Five Key Metrics to Understand:
  1. Bandwidth - Frequency range the channel can pass
  2. Throughput - Actual data rate achieved
  3. Latency/Delay - Time for data to reach destination
  4. Bandwidth-Delay Product - Total data "in flight"
  5. Jitter - Variation in packet delays
📊 1. Bandwidth
In Hz: The range of frequencies a channel can transmit (f_high - f_low)
In bps: The maximum data rate the channel supports (e.g., 100 Mbps for Ethernet)
⚡ 2. Throughput
Definition: The actual rate at which data is successfully transmitted through the network, measured in bits per second (bps). Always ≤ Bandwidth.

⏱️ 3. Latency (Delay)
Definition: Time for the first bit sent from source to reach the destination. It's the sum of four components:
Latency Components:
Latency = Propagation Time + Transmission Time + Queuing Time + Processing Delay

Propagation Time = Distance / Propagation Speed

📦 4. Bandwidth-Delay Product
Definition: The amount of data that is "in flight" on the network at any given time. It represents the channel capacity × the round-trip delay.
Formula:
BW-Delay Product = Bandwidth (bps) × Latency (seconds)
This tells you how many bits can be on the wire simultaneously
📉 5. Jitter
Definition: The variation in delay between consecutive packets. If packet 1 takes 20ms and packet 2 takes 45ms, there's jitter. Critical for real-time applications like video calls.

📚 Quick Reference & Key Formulas

1. Period and Frequency:
Period (T) = 1 / Frequency (F)
F = 1 / T
2. Wavelength:
λ = c / f
where c ≈ 3 × 10⁸ m/s (speed of light)
3. Bandwidth of Composite Signal:
BW = fhighest - flowest
4. Attenuation (Decibels):
dB = 10 log₁₀(P₂/P₁) [Power-based]
dB = 20 log₁₀(V₂/V₁) [Voltage-based]
5. SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio):
SNR (dB) = 10 log₁₀(Psignal/Pnoise)
6. Propagation Time:
Propagation Time = Distance / Speed
7. Throughput:
Throughput = (Frames/min × Bits/frame) / 60
8. Total Latency:
Latency = Propagation + Transmission + Queuing + Processing
