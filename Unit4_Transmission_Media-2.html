Transmission Media — Overview

A transmission medium is the physical path through which data travels from sender to receiver. It is broadly classified into Guided (Wired) and Unguided (Wireless) media.

DEFINITION

A Transmission Medium is a physical or logical channel that enables data signals to travel from a transmitter to a receiver. The nature of the medium determines the speed, bandwidth, and reliability of communication.

🔌

Guided Media (Wired)

Signal travels through a physical conductor — twisted pair, coaxial, or fiber optic cables. Best for short to medium distances.

📡

Unguided Media (Wireless)

Signal propagates through the air as electromagnetic waves — radio, microwave, or infrared. Suitable for long-distance and mobile communication.

🔀

Switching

Process of transferring data packets from one device/network to another using switches — circuit, packet, or message switching.

⚙️

Switching Devices

Electronic components (routers, bridges, switches, hubs) that direct data flow across networks and between network segments.

📶

OSI Layer

Switching primarily occurs at the Data Link Layer (Layer 2) of the OSI model, immediately after signal generation at the Physical Layer.

⚡

Signal Types

Guided media carries signals as voltage, current, or photons. Unguided media carries signals as electromagnetic waves.

Guided Media

Twisted Pair Cable

Two copper conductors twisted together to minimize electromagnetic interference (EMI) and crosstalk. One wire carries signal; the other serves as ground reference.

DEFINITION

A Twisted Pair Cable consists of two insulated copper conductors twisted around each other. The twisting cancels out electromagnetic interference by ensuring that noise picked up on one wire is equal and opposite on the other, thus nullifying the effect — a principle known as differential signaling.

🛡️

UTP — Unshielded Twisted Pair

No additional shielding. Used in telephone lines, Ethernet LANs (Cat5, Cat6). Affordable and flexible.

⚔️

STP — Shielded Twisted Pair

Has a metal foil/braid shield around pairs. Better noise protection. Used in industrial environments.

📞

Applications

Telephone lines (PSTN), DSL internet connections, LAN (Ethernet), local loop in telephone offices.

Guided Media

Coaxial Cable

A coaxial cable uses a central conductor surrounded by insulation, a metallic shield, and an outer plastic jacket. It provides excellent shielding and can carry high-frequency signals.

DEFINITION

A Coaxial Cable (coax) consists of a central copper conductor, surrounded by a dielectric insulator, enclosed by a tubular conducting shield, and wrapped in an outer plastic jacket. The coaxial geometry allows it to carry high-frequency signals with low loss and excellent EMI resistance. Originally used for 10,000 voice signals in analog telephony; later carried up to 600 Mbps in digital networks.

📺

Cable TV (CATV)

Delivers television signals over 75Ω RG-6 coaxial cable to homes.

🌐

Internet / Broadband

Used in cable internet (DOCSIS standard) for high-speed broadband connections.

📻

Radio / Antenna

Connects radio transmitters and receivers to antennas in RF systems.

Guided Media

Fiber Optic Cable

Uses light pulses to transmit data through thin glass or plastic fibers using total internal reflection. Fastest guided medium with virtually no electromagnetic interference.

DEFINITION

A Fiber Optic Cable transmits data as pulses of light through a glass or plastic core, surrounded by a cladding with a lower refractive index. The light undergoes total internal reflection, bouncing along the length of the fiber. It supports extremely high bandwidths (Tbps), immune to EMI, and is used for long-distance, high-speed data transmission.

💡

Single-Mode Fiber

Very narrow core (~9μm). Light travels in a straight line. Used for long distances (telecom backbone). Laser light source.

🌈

Multi-Mode Fiber

Wider core (~50-62.5μm). Multiple light paths. Used for shorter distances (campus networks). LED light source.

🏥

Applications

Telecom backbone, medical (endoscopy), CCTV, internet backbone, defense systems, automobile sensors.

Unguided Media

Unguided (Wireless) Media

Signals propagate through the atmosphere as electromagnetic waves — no physical conductor required. Three main types: Radio waves, Microwaves, and Infrared waves.

DEFINITION

Unguided Media (wireless / unbounded transmission media) transmits data as electromagnetic waves through the air or vacuum without any physical conductor. The signals travel omni-directionally or uni-directionally depending on the wave type. It forms continuous network topologies and is essential for mobile, satellite, and broadcast communication.

Switching

Circuit Switching

A dedicated communication path is established between two nodes before data transmission begins. The path remains reserved for the entire duration of the connection, then released when done.

DEFINITION

Circuit Switching establishes a dedicated, end-to-end physical circuit between the communicating parties before any data is transferred. This circuit (set of links) is exclusively reserved for the duration of the session. It involves three phases: Circuit Establishment → Data Transfer → Circuit Teardown. Traditional telephone networks (PSTN) use circuit switching.

🔗

Phase 1: Setup

A dedicated path is reserved through the network before transmission. Resources allocated from source to destination.

📤

Phase 2: Transfer

Data flows continuously over the dedicated circuit at full reserved bandwidth. No congestion, guaranteed QoS.

🔓

Phase 3: Teardown

After transmission ends, the circuit is released and resources freed for other connections.

Switching

Packet Switching

Data is divided into small packets, each routed independently through the network. Packets may take different paths and are reassembled at the destination. No dedicated circuit required.

DEFINITION

Packet Switching breaks data into fixed or variable-size packets. Each packet contains a header (source, destination, sequence number) and is routed independently. Routers make per-packet forwarding decisions. The internet uses packet switching. Two approaches: Datagram (connectionless) and Virtual Circuit (connection-oriented).

✂️

Datagram Approach

Each packet is treated independently. No fixed path. Packets may arrive out of order. Used in the internet (IP).

🛣️

Virtual Circuit

A logical path is set up but not exclusively reserved. Packets follow the same path but share bandwidth with others.

📦

Packet Structure

Header (source IP, dest IP, seq no., TTL) + Payload (actual data) + Trailer (error check).

Switching

Message Switching

Entire messages are stored at each intermediate node and then forwarded to the next node — a "store-and-forward" mechanism. No dedicated path, but entire message stored at each hop.

DEFINITION

Message Switching uses the store-and-forward principle where an entire message is received, stored in a buffer, and then forwarded to the next node when the outgoing link is free. Unlike packet switching, messages are not divided into packets. Email communication is the classic example of message switching at the application layer.

💾

Store & Forward

Each intermediate node stores the complete message before forwarding it. Requires large storage buffers.

✉️

Example: Email

SMTP servers store and forward email messages. Messages may be queued if the next server is busy.

⏱️

Delay

Higher latency than circuit or packet switching due to store-and-forward at each node. Not suitable for real-time.

Switching Devices

Switching Devices

Electronic components used to open/close circuits and redirect data across network segments. A small control signal manages much larger currents or voltages automatically.

DEFINITION

Switching Devices are electronic or networking components that direct data traffic between network nodes, segments, or networks. They "switch" data flows on/off or redirect them without direct human intervention, using control signals. They operate at various OSI layers and include hubs, bridges, switches, and routers.

🔵

Hub (Layer 1)

Broadcasts all incoming data to every port. No filtering. Causes collisions. Operates at Physical Layer. Now obsolete.

🟢

Bridge (Layer 2)

Connects two network segments. Filters frames using MAC addresses. Reduces collision domains. Operates at Data Link Layer.

🟡

Switch (Layer 2)

Multi-port bridge with MAC address table. Sends frames only to the destination port. Eliminates collisions. Standard LAN device.

🔴

Router (Layer 3)

Routes packets between different networks using IP addresses. Connects LANs to WANs. Intelligent path selection.

🔗

Repeater

Amplifies/regenerates signal to extend network distance. Layer 1 device. No filtering capability.

🌐

Gateway

Connects networks with different protocols. Operates at all layers. Translates between different network architectures.
